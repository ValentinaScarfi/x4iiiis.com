Global Game Jam

My Profile


Artists, musicians/sound engineers, coders and general game enthusiasts from around the world unite to spend one entire weekend each year making small games. Every January, people take part in a 48-hour challenge in which they must create a game - either solo or in a team - relative to a theme announced at the start of the event. Once the theme has been announced, entrants engage in 48 hours of accelerated (rushed) game development. Sleep is optional.

Global Game Jam is the world's largest event of its kind, and takes place in several physical locations around the globe, referred to as 'jam sites'. In my case, that is Edinburgh Napier University.

Ludum Dare

My Profile


Much like Global Game Jam, Ludum Dare - Latin for "to give a game" - is another long-running, largely popular accelerated game development event.

This time however, creators can take up to 72 hours to make their game. That said, there is a more competitive version of the event whereby there are stricter rules to abide by, and only 48 hours in which to do so.

These events run much the same way as GGJ does, but Ludum Dare isn't just an annual event; It takes place every April, August, and December.

NeuroSwitch

NeuroSwitch was my Honours Project in my final year at university. I'll write a wee bit about it here, but it does have its own dedicated page.

Advanced Games Engineering I

Given a minimalistic project solution, we were tasked with creating a shoot 'em up game using SFML and C++.

The timeframe was a week, and the result was pretty pish.

Advanced Games Engineering II

A poor group-working experience lead to an incomplete game that had initially showed good promise. I was hugely disappointed with the result, and so I took it upon myself to continue development in my spare time in a bid to keep the idea alive.

The idea was to create a shoot 'em up style game, but with a twist. That twist was that after a set amount of time, the game would transform into a platformer after the player takes on a boss. Then, at the end of the platformer would be another boss battle.

After the module, the shoot 'em up stage wasn't even completed - even without a final boss. It was a real mess and became a scramble to tick boxes to earn marks for the coursework rather than being about what we wanted the game to be.

As a result, a lot of work needed to be done, but here is the final result!

Giveaway Application

I'd never worked with an API, and I'd never worked with Python. Therefore, I decided to work with an API using Python!

Actually, it was two APIs. I wrote (naw ye didny) an application to find out who tweeted a given set of keywords, or full sentence.

That way, when I decide to do a giveaway on my YouTube channel, I can tell people to get creative with a set of keywords and try to make a funny sentence or tweet. Then, I'll find all of these tweets with my script, and enter their @handle into the prize draw!
I'll probably automatically retweet and/or like them all too.

I also want to be able to see who has both liked and shared a facebook post announcing the giveaway. These people will also be entered into the draw. People with both twitter and facebook accounts will be able to enter the draw twice, or even more if they have multiple accounts.

There's not really a lot I can do about that - but hey, it's more exposure for me :p
